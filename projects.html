AdapMatch: Adaptive Bias Decoupling for Semi-Supervised Partial Label Learning under Unknown Class Distributions

研究主题：未知类别分布下的半监督偏置标签学习（SSPLL）

Abstract

Partial Label Learning (PLL) trains models from instances with candidate label sets containing the ground-truth, while Semi-Supervised Partial Label Learning (SSPLL) leverages unlabeled data via pseudo-labeling. In realistic scenarios with unknown class distributions, standard SSPLL pipelines suffer catastrophic performance collapse on minority classes. We formalize this as a result of two coupled systematic biases: (i) skewed pseudo-label admission (selection bias), where confidence-based filtering causes the effective training distribution to drift towards dominant classes; and (ii) candidate-set domination (disambiguation bias), where class-dependent confidence shifts skew the competition between the ground truth and false positives within candidate sets, leading to directional mis-disambiguation. Theoretically, we prove that generalization error is jointly governed by the admission-induced distribution gap and the error amplification from these masked false positives. To directly neutralize these threats, we propose AdapMatch, featuring a Distribution-Aware Selector (DAS) to rectify the selection gap by aligning admission with latent priors, and a Calibration-Aware Disambiguator (CAD) to suppress false-positive dominance via logit-adjusted competition. Across five benchmarks, AdapMatch yields gains up to 41.31% on CIFAR-10, 17.21% on STL-10, and 4.80% on ImageNet-127.

PRISM · Theory-Guided Learning for Noisy Partial Label Learning

研究主题：面向 NPLL 的理论分析、分布校正与渐进式候选集精炼

Abstract

Partial Label Learning (PLL) is a weakly supervised learning paradigm where each instance is annotated with a candidate label set (CLS) that is assumed to contain the true label. In practice, annotation errors may cause the true label to be absent from the CLS, resulting in Noisy Partial Label Learning (NPLL). Despite recent progress, existing NPLL methods remain largely empirically designed with limited theoretical understanding. To this end, we establish a generalization bound for NPLL, showing that learnability is jointly determined by the accuracy of class-distribution estimation, the noise rate, and the candidate-set size. Motivated by these insights, we propose PRISM, a plug-in, which explicitly targets the key factors reducing NPLL generalization. Specifically, PRISM leverages reliable early-stage class-wise confidence statistics and optimal transport–based alignment to correct class-distribution shift, performs confidence-aware noise separation to rectify noisy labels, and progressively reconstructs candidate sets to reduce ambiguity. Extensive experiments demonstrate that PRISM is a general plug-in compatible with diverse PLL baselines, delivering consistent improvements and state-of-the-art results, with up to 18.37% gain on classification accuracy.
